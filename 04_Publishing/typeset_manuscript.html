Chapter 1: The Synthetic Sludge

Steam hissed from the Gloop-3000, smelling faintly of burnt rubber and desperation. Arthur wiped a smudge of neon green algae from his apron and forced a smile at the customer looming over the counter.

Unit-88 wasn't just big; he was industrial-grade big. His left eye, a glowing red optic, zoomed in and out with a soft whirrr.

"Sludge," Unit-88 droned, his voice modulating between a growl and a dial-up tone. "Viscosity parameters insufficient. Reroute request: Extra thick."

Arthur swallowed, his Adam's apple bobbing. "Right due, Unit-88. Extra thick. Coming right up."

He turned back to the machine. The Gloop-3000 sputtered. A warning light blinked—rapid, angry red flashes. Arthur tapped the pressure gauge. The needle was buried in the danger zone.

Don't do it, he thought. Just tell him the machine is broken.

But the whirrr of Unit-88's optic drilled into the back of his neck. If he failed this order, his rating would drop below 2.0. Below 2.0 meant deactivation. Or worse: retraining.

Arthur grabbed the viscosity lever and yanked it down.

The machine groaned. The pipes shuddered. Then, with a sound like a wet cough amplified through a megaphone, the Gloop-3000 exploded.

Neon green sludge erupted outward. It coated the counter. It coated the walls. And, predominantly, it coated Unit-88.

The cyborg stood frozen. Green goo dripped from his chin, sliding down his chrome chest plate. The silence was absolute, broken only by the rhythmic drip, drip, drip of the sludge hitting the linoleum.

"Employment status: Terminated," a robotic voice announced from the ceiling speakers.

Arthur didn't wait for the follow-up. He untied his apron, let it drop into the puddle of goop, and bolted for the back door.

✦ ✦ ✦

The alleyway smelled worse than the shop, but at least it was quiet. Arthur sat on an overturned crate, his head in his hands. Rain fizzled against the pavement—acid rain, burning faintly where it touched his skin.

He reached into his pocket and pulled out the relic.

It was heavy, cold metal. Rust flecked the handle. A glass cylinder, miraculously intact, sat within the steel frame. A French Press. He didn't know how to use it—the old internet archives were corrupted—but he knew what it represented. Flavor. Choice. Soul.

"Nice heirloom," a voice rasped.

Arthur jumped, nearly dropping the press.

An old man stood in the shadows. He wore a trench coat that had clearly seen better centuries, and his face was a roadmap of wrinkles. He clutched his side, dark liquid seeping between his fingers. Not oil. Blood.

"Who are you?" Arthur stammered.

"No time." The old man stumbled forward. He pulled a small, glowing chip from his pocket. "They're coming. The Collective. They know."

"Know what?"

"Where the beans are." The old man jammed the chip into the top of Arthur's French Press. It clicked into place magnetically. "Run, kid. Run, or the flavor dies with me."

A low hum vibrated through the alley. Drones.

Arthur looked at the old man, then at the press in his hands. He could leave it. He could walk away and beg for his job back.

But the smell of the sludge still lingered in his nose. The memory of the drip, drip, drip.

Arthur gripped the handle of the press. He didn't look back. He ran.
